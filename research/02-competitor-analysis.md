## Patterns of the Top 10%

These 5 elements separate elite freight websites from average ones:

### Pattern 1: Compliance Badge Trio (The Trust Stack)
Every high-scoring site prominently displays CTPAT, FAST, PIP, or equivalent certifications above the fold or immediately visible. These are the B2B equivalent of "trust seals" on e-commerce sites. Shippers' procurement teams look for these before anything else.

**Keylink opportunity:** Feature USDOT# and MC# more prominently. Pursue CTPAT certification if not already held — and display it front and center.

### Pattern 2: Hard Statistics Before Soft Claims
Top sites lead with numbers, not adjectives. "Over 30 years" beats "experienced." "500-700 border crossings daily" beats "cross-border specialists." "650+ units" beats "large fleet."

**Keylink opportunity:** Feature: Founded 2019 → 5+ years of operational data, shipments delivered, on-time rate %, miles driven, routes covered.

### Pattern 3: Background Video Heroes
First Frontier, and multiple best-in-class examples, use full-width highway/truck video in the hero. This immediately communicates operational scale and creates emotional resonance that static images can't match.

**Keylink opportunity:** Hero video (or high-quality GSAP animation of trucks/routes) is standard at the $12K price point.

### Pattern 4: Dual-Audience Navigation
Every top site clearly separates the experience for shippers vs. drivers/carriers in navigation. Nishan, Schneider, and J.B. Hunt are examples. Without this, driver recruitment and shipper conversion interfere with each other.

**Keylink opportunity:** Separate "Ship Freight" and "Join Our Team" pathways visually in the nav and hero.

### Pattern 5: Named, Industry-Specific Testimonials
Anonymous "great company!" reviews signal a small operator. Named customers ("Tier 1 Automotive Company", "Tool & Mold Company") signal that serious businesses trust you.

**Keylink opportunity:** Convert Glassdoor reviews and broker feedback into properly attributed testimonials. Even "5-star rated by industry brokers" should be a quote from a named broker.
